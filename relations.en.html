No religion appeared out of nowhere. Judaism gave birth to Christianity, and both form the backdrop of Islam's self-understanding; the Vedic tradition and the śramaṇa movement grew into Hinduism, Jainism and Buddhism; Buddhism, moving east, interpenetrated Daoism, Confucianism and Shinto. This map gathers the links scattered across each issue's "cross-tradition bridge" into one network — solid lines are descent, dashed lines are influence, double-headed lines are mutual absorption.

Reading the map

Four families, three kinds of ties

  • One Abrahamic line: Christianity emerged from a messianic movement within Judaism (1st century); Islam recognizes and recasts the Judeo-Christian line of prophets (7th century). Christianity split twice: East and West in 1054 (Catholic / Orthodox), and the 16th-century Reformation (Protestantism). Sufism is Islam's inner mystical current; the Baha'i Faith arose in the 19th century out of a Shia milieu via the Bábí movement, then established itself as an independent religion. Zoroastrianism influenced Second-Temple Judaism's eschatology and angelology — the degree is debated among scholars, hence a dashed line.
  • India's two springs: the Vedic tradition evolved continuously into Hinduism; the śramaṇa movement (a response to Vedic ritualism) produced Jainism and Buddhism. Hinduism and Buddhism then absorbed from each other on the subcontinent for a millennium. Sikhism arose in 15th-century Punjab from soil that included the Hindu bhakti movement and Sufi thought — the Sikh tradition itself emphasizes independent revelation; the two dashed lines mark context, not derivation.
  • East Asian interpenetration: Buddhism entered China around the 1st century and interacted with Daoism and Confucianism for centuries, the three settling into the "three teachings" folk-religious ecology; in Japan it fused with Shinto as shinbutsu-shūgō (the norm until the Meiji government forced separation in 1868).
  • Diaspora and fusion: Yoruba and other West African traditions crossed the Atlantic with the slave trade and fused with colonial Catholicism, producing diaspora religions such as Vodou (Haiti) and Candomblé (Brazil).
What is deliberately not drawn

The restraint

  • Buddhism appears because without it the Indian and East Asian networks fall apart; its deep reading lives not here but on the Buddhism site (click the node).
  • Indigenous and shamanic traditions (D19) are not drawn: they are multi-origin traditions spread across every continent, with no single lineage line to draw — forcing one would distort.
  • The comparative topics (D21–28) are cross-cutting perspectives, not nodes.
  • The "background sources" (Vedic tradition, śramaṇa movement) get no Day of their own: they are the intellectual soil of a formative era, not living traditions with adherents and distributions today — the four-dimension card (history / distribution / beliefs / practice) simply does not fit them. The Vedic evolution is told inside D9 Hinduism; the śramaṇa story lives in D11 Jainism and in "Turning Points" below.
  • Only scholarly-recognized strong links are drawn; surface resemblances ("they all teach love and compassion") do not make an edge. The map is a starting point, not the whole story — for each line's detail and disputes, see the "cross-tradition bridge" of the corresponding Day.
Turning points · the forks and meetings on the map

Each junction: who, what happened, and how it ended

The messianic movement1st century

WhoJesus of Nazareth and his disciples; Paul carried the movement into the Greco-Roman world.

WhatAfter Jesus was executed by the Roman authorities around 30 CE, his followers proclaimed him risen — the Messiah; the Council of Jerusalem (c. 49) adopted Paul's position that gentile converts need not first become Jews through circumcision.

OutcomeThe movement spilled beyond Judaism; after the Temple's destruction (70) and the Bar Kokhba revolt (132–135) the two faiths parted ways — by the 2nd century Christianity was a religion of its own.

The Sunni–Shia succession disputefrom 632

WhoThe Prophet Muhammad's companions; two lines of succession — Abu Bakr and Ali.

WhatMuhammad died in 632 without an undisputed succession arrangement: the majority acclaimed Abu Bakr as caliph (the Sunni narrative), while some held that the Prophet had designated his cousin and son-in-law Ali (the Shia narrative). In 680 Ali's son Husayn was killed at Karbala.

OutcomeA political disagreement hardened into a confessional divide — Sunni ~85–90%, Shia ~10–15% (today concentrated in Iran, Iraq and elsewhere); Karbala became the heart of Shia martyrdom memory.

The Great Schism1054

WhoCardinal Humbert, legate of Pope Leo IX, vs Patriarch Michael I Cerularius of Constantinople.

WhatCenturies of friction over the filioque clause, papal primacy and liturgical differences; in 1054 the two sides exchanged excommunications in Hagia Sophia. The Fourth Crusade's sack of Constantinople (1204) made the rift final.

OutcomeWestern Catholicism and Eastern Orthodoxy stand apart to this day; the mutual anathemas were lifted in 1965, but communion has not been restored.

The Reformationfrom 1517

WhoMartin Luther (Wittenberg), Zwingli (Zurich), Calvin (Geneva), among others.

WhatIn 1517 Luther published the Ninety-five Theses against indulgences, championing sola scriptura and justification by faith; the printing press swept the debate across Europe; Henry VIII set up a separate Church of England.

OutcomeThe Protestant denominations were born; the Peace of Augsburg (1555) established "whose realm, his religion," and the Peace of Westphalia (1648) ended the wars of religion, largely fixing Europe's confessional map.

The Bábí movement → Baha'i1844–1863

WhoSiyyid Ali-Muhammad of Shiraz, who took the title "the Báb" ("the Gate"); his follower Bahá'u'lláh.

WhatIn 1844 the Báb proclaimed a new revelation in Shia Iran; the movement was suppressed and the Báb executed in 1850. In 1863 Bahá'u'lláh declared himself the one the Báb had foretold — "He whom God shall make manifest."

OutcomeThe Baha'i Faith established itself as an independent religion (not an Islamic sect), teaching the common origin of religions and progressive revelation; its adherents remain persecuted in Iran.

The śramaṇa movementc. 6th–5th century BCE

WhoRenouncer-ascetics of the Ganges basin; Mahavira (Vardhamana) and the Buddha proved the most consequential.

WhatIn an age of urbanization and rival kingdoms, renouncers questioned the efficacy of Vedic sacrifice and the authority of the Brahmins, turning instead to austerity, meditation and paths of liberation.

OutcomeJainism and Buddhism grew out of it; it also pushed the Vedic tradition to reform from within (the Upanishads' karma, rebirth and liberation), shaping the Hinduism to come.

Buddhism enters China; the three teachingsfrom the 1st century

WhoSilk Road merchants and translator-monks — An Shigao, Kumārajīva (arrived Chang'an 401), Xuanzang (7th-century journey west).

WhatBuddhism arrived around the Eastern Han (the White Horse Temple legend is dated 68 CE); through centuries of translation it competed with and absorbed from Daoism and Confucianism, developing sinicized schools such as Chan and Pure Land; the Huichang persecution (845) was its greatest setback.

OutcomeThe East Asian Mahayana ecology took shape; from the Song onward the "three teachings in one" became the substrate of folk religion.

Shinbutsu-shūgō and the Meiji separationfrom the 8th c. / 1868

WhoThe Japanese court and the Buddhist sangha; later, the new Meiji government.

WhatFrom the Nara period, kami were paired with buddhas and bodhisattvas (honji suijaku); shrines and temples formed one fabric for nearly a millennium. In 1868 the Meiji government decreed the separation of kami and buddhas, triggering a wave of temple destruction (haibutsu kishaku) and the gradual construction of State Shinto.

OutcomeShinto and Buddhism were institutionally separated (State Shinto was dismantled in 1945); popular practice still often runs on both tracks.

The Atlantic crossing and colonial fusion16th–19th centuries

WhoWest Africans — Yoruba, Fon, Kongo and other peoples — taken to the Americas by the slave trade.

WhatSome 12.5 million people were shipped across the Atlantic; under forced Catholicization, believers mapped the orisha and other spirits onto Catholic saints, carrying ancestral worship inside the shell of saints' feast days.

OutcomeThe diaspora religions — Vodou (Haiti), Candomblé (Brazil), Santería (Cuba) — were born, and remain alive today.
