World Religions

Jainism 耆那教

Day 11Dharmic traditions~4–6 million followers

A small religion of only a few million people pushed a single idea to the very edge of human imagination: do no harm. Where other traditions say "do not kill people," Jainism asks — does an insect count? A blade of grass? A microbe in the soil? Taking that question all the way to the end carves out a singular path of faith: radical non-violence (ahimsa), a view of the soul as a prisoner bound by matter, and a form of end-of-life practice so rare it will let a person fast unto death.

"Souls render service to one another."—— Tattvartha Sutra 5:21, parasparopagraho jivanam, the motto of Jainism

History & Origins

Not founded, but "rediscovered"

Jains do not think of their faith as something invented by one person in one year. In their cosmology, truth is revealed again and again across vast stretches of time by a long line of tirthankaras ("ford-makers" — those who build a crossing over the ocean of birth and death); there are 24 such enlightened ones in the present era, an ancient lineage of sages standing apart from the Vedic mainstream. The first 22 belong to myth and deep antiquity; only the last two are historically attestable.

The 23rd, Parshvanatha, is generally placed by historians in the 8th–7th century BCE around Varanasi. He laid down a "fourfold restraint" — no killing, no lying, no stealing, no possessing — the earliest recoverable frame of Jain ethics. The 24th and last ford-maker of this era, Mahavira (born Vardhamana), is traditionally dated 599–527 BCE, though most scholars favor the later 5th century BCE — a near-contemporary of the Buddha in the same Ganges basin. To Parshvanatha's four restraints he added brahmacharya (chastity) as a fifth vow and drove non-violence to an unprecedented extreme. Mahavira was not a "founder" but the tradition's reformer and consolidator.

Distribution Today

Very few in number, heavy in weight

Jainism has one of the smallest followings among the world's major religions. Global estimates run to about 4–6 million (sources vary with how "religion vs. ethnic identity" is defined); India's 2011 census recorded roughly 4.45 million, about 0.4% of the population, concentrated in the central-western states of Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Gujarat and Karnataka. Overseas communities — several hundred thousand in all — are mainly Indian-origin populations in the UK, US, Canada and East Africa.

Worth naming is the tradition's paradox: less than 0.4% of the population, yet a social weight far beyond its numbers. Because non-violence is observed so strictly, farming (which harms creatures in the soil), fishing, hunting and butchery are all avoided; historically Jains clustered in trade, finance, jewelry and law — "bloodless" callings — becoming one of India's most literate and prosperous communities, and among its most active in charity (animal shelters, free clinics, education). It is a classic ethnic-transmitted rather than universalizing-missionary religion: it almost never proselytizes, passing down by lineage, which is why its numbers have held steady at a small scale for millennia.

Core Belief System

The soul is light tangled in matter

Jainism is a religion without a creator god: there is no world-maker; the cosmos is beginningless, endless and self-running. It divides all that exists into two kinds — the sentient jiva (soul) and the insentient ajiva (matter and the like). The crux lies in how it understands karma: here karma is not an abstract moral ledger but an extremely subtle form of matter that, through your actions (above all violence and attachment), clings to the soul like dust — weighing down, clouding and trapping in rebirth a soul that ought to be luminous and all-knowing.

So the logic of liberation (moksha) is strikingly "physical": not to beg a god's pardon, but to first stop the inflow of new karma, then grind away the old, layer by layer, until the soul is wholly cleansed and rises to the summit of the universe as a siddha (perfected being), forever free of birth and death. Jainism also gave world philosophy a gift: anekantavada (the doctrine of many-sidedness) — truth has many facets, and any single viewpoint captures only a part. The classic image is the blind men and the elephant: the one touching a leg calls it a pillar, the one touching an ear calls it a fan; each grasps one aspect, none the whole. From this grew syadvada ("perhaps"-qualified assertion): every claim should be prefaced with "from a certain point of view…". It is a bone-deep epistemic humility, often read as Jain non-violence extended into the realm of thought — even asserting should not be domineering.

Principles & Practice

Turning "do no harm" into a discipline

Jain practice runs on two levels: monastics keep the full "great vows" (mahavrata); lay householders keep the workable, scaled-down "small vows" (anuvrata). Almost every daily observance grows from the single taproot of non-violence:

  • Strict vegetarianism, and then some: no meat, no eggs; many also avoid onions, garlic and potatoes and other root vegetables — because uprooting kills the whole plant and harms countless microbes on the roots. The devout do not eat after dark, lest they swallow tiny insects drawn to the lamp.
  • The monastic extreme: mendicant monks sweep the ground ahead with a soft broom, wear a cloth over the mouth to avoid inhaling or crushing minute life, and filter their water first. Digambara ("sky-clad") monks carry non-possession to its limit and go naked; Svetambara ("white-robed") monastics wear white and hold that women too can attain liberation in this very body — one of the most visible differences between the two sects.
  • Festival and forgiveness: the yearly Paryushana (Das Lakshana among Digambaras) is the most important festival — fasting, recitation, self-examination; on its closing "day of forgiveness" the faithful say to one another Micchami Dukkadam ("may my wrongdoing be forgiven"), actively asking pardon of all beings they may have harmed.
  • The contested end-of-life practice sallekhana / santhara: a very few, at life's close and when the body is in irreversible decline, choose to gradually reduce food and meet death calmly — seen as the crowning of a life of non-violence (releasing even attachment to one's own body). It is distinguished from suicide (no passion, no hatred, requiring a teacher's sanction and revocable at any time), but has sparked legal debate in modern India — briefly ruled unlawful by a state high court in 2015, then stayed by the Supreme Court, with no final resolution to date.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: Jainism is a branch of Buddhism.
The two arose in the same era (5th century BCE) and the same Ganges region, both rejecting Vedic sacrificial authority, so they are often confused; but they are independent traditions. Jainism affirms that the soul is real, that karma is subtle matter, and seeks liberation through extreme asceticism — a direct contrast with Buddhism's core teachings of "no-self" and the "middle way."

Misconception: non-violence just means "don't fight, don't kill."
In Jainism, ahimsa is an entire way of life saturating food, occupation, speech and even thought — even unintended harm to microbes is to be minimized, and even a dogmatic assertion is regarded as a kind of mental violence. It is an active discipline, not a passive prohibition.

Misconception: Jains all go naked, mortify themselves and starve to death.
Nakedness is confined to Digambara monks, and sallekhana is a rare choice of a very few at life's end. The vast majority of Jains are laypeople living modern urban lives — in business, professions and philanthropy — keeping the workable "small vows," not the monastic extreme.

Misconception: a religion that pushes non-violence to the limit must be passive and world-fleeing.
Quite the opposite. Precisely because farming, herding and butchery are avoided, Jain communities went deep into commerce and charity, and are strikingly active in animal welfare, environmentalism, vegetarian ethics and peace movements — Gandhi's philosophy of non-violence was deeply shaped by Jain friends and mentors.

Cross-tradition reading · Contemporary context

One soil, three answers. Jainism, Buddhism (covered in the deeper Buddhist-studies track elsewhere) and Hinduism (Days 9–10) share the vocabulary of karma, rebirth and liberation, yet answer differently: Hinduism largely by devotion, duty and yoga; Buddhism by the "no-self" middle way; Jainism by holding the soul to be real and requiring one to shed karmic dust through severe self-discipline. Set side by side, they show most clearly how human thought forks under one shared set of questions.

A modern echo of non-violence. Jainism deeply influenced Gandhi — his close friend and spiritual mentor Srimad Rajchandra was a Jain; passed from Gandhi on to Martin Luther King Jr., the non-violent resistance movement let this small religion's central idea indirectly help rewrite 20th-century world history.

Today's tensions. Contemporary Jain communities are pulled by two sets of questions: the legality of sallekhana — how a traditional end-of-life practice sits with modern legal notions of the right to life; and ecology and animal ethics — in an age of climate change and industrial livestock farming, the ancient ahimsa is being rediscovered by many as a forward-looking environmental and vegetarian ethic, while diaspora communities keep grappling with how to transmit these demanding disciplines to the next generation.

Deeper Reflection

If even breathing and walking may harm microbes, is "total non-violence" simply impossible?
Jainism sees this clearly: perfect harmlessness is unattainable while the body persists — which is the deep reason it pushes monastic asceticism to the extreme and rounds off a life with sallekhana. The logical endpoint of total non-violence is to relinquish even the violence of sustaining oneself. But for the vast majority of laypeople, the point is not the unreachable limit of "zero harm" but a sustained intention to minimize harm: avoiding harm by degrees according to a being's level of sense-perception (from single-sense plants and microbes up to five-sense animals and humans), harming as little as possible. It offers not a finish line one can cross, but a direction that forces constant weighing.
Many-sidedness grants that truth has many facets — does this slide into "anything goes" relativism?
This is the most common charge against anekantavada, but it differs from relativism. Relativism says "there is no objective truth"; many-sidedness says "there is objective truth, but it is too rich for any single viewpoint to capture more than one facet" — the elephant really exists, only the leg-toucher and the ear-toucher each see one part. So it demands not the abandonment of judgment but labeling each judgment with its viewpoint and staying open to others' (that is exactly what syadvada does). It is closer to an epistemic humility and non-violence than to abolishing right and wrong. Critics still press: when viewpoints conflict, on what basis do we integrate them? That remains an open philosophical problem.
How has a religion that barely proselytizes, with so few adherents, survived for over two millennia?
Jainism offers a case study in being "small yet undying." It thrives not by expansion but on a few structural supports: tight ethnic and family transmission (identity nearly co-extensive with birth), economic success and highly organized charity (the community self-supports and supports its own), and a clear, distinctive identity boundary (strict diet and discipline are themselves powerful glue). Moreover, the division of monks and laity lets a very high ideal be carried by a few renunciants while most keep a workable version — the ideal is neither diluted nor imposed on everyone. This model of "high standard, small numbers, strong bonds" is the key to its steady survival through many changes of empire.
