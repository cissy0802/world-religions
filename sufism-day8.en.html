Sufism

Day 8Abrahamic · IslamA mystical dimension cutting across Sunni and Shia

Sufism is not a "sect" of Islam but its inward dimension—the effort to turn "God is one" from a creedal statement into a lived nearness and love realized with the whole self. A counter-intuitive fact: the best-selling poet in many Western bookstores is, year after year, the thirteenth-century Sufi Rumi—and this tradition, romanticized as "gentle spirituality," has produced both the most rigorous of orthodox theologians and a man executed for saying "I am the Truth." Today, in some places, it is even the target of violent attack.

"We are closer to him than his jugular vein."—— Qur'an 50:16, which the Sufi tradition reads as evidence that God's presence is nearer than the self itself

History & Origins

From a coarse wool cloak to the path of love

The word "Sufi" most likely derives from the Arabic suf (wool)—early ascetics wore rough woolen cloaks to signal their renunciation of luxury; the discipline itself is called tasawwuf. Another account traces it to the "people of the bench" (ahl al-suffa), a group of poor companions of the Prophet who gathered to remember God.

Its roots lie in the asceticism (zuhd) of the 8th century CE. In an age when the Umayyad empire grew ever wealthier and more ostentatious, some turned the other way, stressing awe, simplicity, and interiority; Hasan al-Basri of Basra is the emblematic figure. The decisive turn came from a woman—Rabia al-Adawiyya of Basra (8th century): she moved asceticism beyond "fear of hell and hope of heaven" into pure love—loving God not out of dread, nor for reward, but because God is worthy of love in Himself. "Love" became the core vocabulary of Sufism thereafter.

The 9th and 10th centuries form the classical period, holding two temperaments in tension. Al-Junayd (d. 910) represents the "sober" school: after the experience of union with God, one returns to the Law and to daily life, keeping one's bearings. Al-Hallaj (d. 922) represents the "intoxicated" school: in a state of self-loss he cried out "I am the Truth" (Ana al-Haqq—"the Truth" being one of the names of God), was charged with blasphemy, and was executed in Baghdad—becoming Sufism's most famous martyr, and the extreme footnote to the tension between "experience" and "the Law."

What brought Sufism into the mainstream was the great synthesizer al-Ghazali (d. 1111): originally a leading jurist, he turned to Sufism after a crisis of faith and wrote The Revival of the Religious Sciences, arguing that inner practice is the fulfilment of Islamic law, not a departure from it. After him, the theory reached its heights—Ibn Arabi (d. 1240) proposed "the unity of being" (wahdat al-wujud), and Rumi (d. 1273) wrote the metaphysics of love into enduring literature in his Persian epic, the Masnavi. Meanwhile, over the 12th–13th centuries, once-loose circles of master and disciples crystallized into "orders" (tariqa, literally "the path"): centered on a sheikh (master) and transmitting their method through a "golden chain" of lineage (silsila) traced back to the Prophet. Sufism now had an organizational skeleton.

Distribution Today

Hard to count, yet almost everywhere

Sufism is not a sect standing alongside Sunni and Shia, but a mystical dimension that cuts across both—the vast majority of Sufis are, doctrinally, Sunni. For that very reason, "how many Sufis are there" admits no hard number; estimates swing across an extremely wide range depending on definition:

  • Narrow count: counting only those formally initiated into an order and practicing its devotions, some put it at about 5% of Muslims.
  • Broad count: including all those shaped by Sufi piety, saint-veneration, and poetry and music, estimates run as high as around 300 million, nearly a fifth of Sunnis.

More reliable are Pew's regional surveys of those who say they "belong to a Sufi order," which is highest in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia—about 92% in Senegal, roughly 55% in Chad, over a quarter in many countries; in South Asia, about 26% in Bangladesh, 17% in Pakistan, and 10% in India. The major orders each have their strongholds:

  • Qadiriyya: the oldest and most widespread, spanning the Middle East, South Asia, and Africa.
  • Naqshbandiyya: Central Asia, Turkey, and South Asia, known for strict adherence to the Law and silent remembrance.
  • Chishtiyya: mainly South Asia, famous for the musical devotion known as qawwali.
  • Shadhiliyya: North Africa and Egypt.
  • Tijaniyya and Mouridiyya: the religious and social backbone of West Africa.
  • Mevleviyya: founded by Rumi's followers, the source of Turkey's "whirling dervishes."

The contemporary situation is polarized: on one side, certain strict revivalist currents (such as Salafism) regard shrine-veneration and musical remembrance as heretical innovation, and in some places violent attacks on Sufi shrines have occurred; on the other, Rumi's poetry is endlessly translated and recited in the West, making Sufism the most widely known face of "Islamic spirituality."

Core Belief System

The Law is the shell, the path is the practice, the Real is the goal

Sufism's basic claim is that Islam has an outer (zahir) and an inner (batin) face. Fasting, prayer, and the Law are the necessary shell, but to stop at the shell is to miss the kernel—what faith truly points toward is an intimate presence with God. The classic three-layer model runs as follows:

The goal: excellence and annihilation

Sufis take the "excellence" (ihsan) of a famous prophetic teaching as their charter—"to worship God as though you see Him; for though you do not see Him, He sees you." To that end one labors to purify the self (nafs, the craving, self-regarding ego), until one reaches fana (the annihilation of the self in God) and then baqa (subsistence in God). Ibn Arabi went further with "the unity of being"—only God is truly real, and all things are but His manifestation.

The line drawn against legalism

Sufism is not a new theology, nor does it deny the Law; what it claims is the experiential dimension of Islam. The tension lies precisely here: when someone claims to have "realized" and seems to have overstepped the letter of the Law, literalists grow wary that this is a dangerous transgression. The very difference between the sober and intoxicated schools within Sufism is a difference over where to draw that boundary.

Principles & Practice

Turning "remembrance of God" into a daily discipline

The core act of Sufi practice is dhikr (remembrance)—repeatedly reciting the names of God or short formulas of praise, often with prayer beads (tasbih), whether alone or in company. The aim is to let "remembrance" seep into breath and heartbeat, so that the heart never forgets God. Around it, the orders developed different methods:

  • Collective remembrance and spiritual music (sama, "listening"): the qawwali song of the Chishti order and the whirling of the Mevlevi order both turn dhikr into a form of sound and body. But not every order accepts this—the Naqshbandiyya and others insist on silent remembrance, holding that soundlessness is more focused.
  • The structure of the orders: the initiate (murid, "disciple") pledges allegiance (bay'a) to a sheikh (Persian: pir), joining a "golden chain" of lineage (silsila) traced back to the Prophet, and progresses step by step under the master's direction. The master-disciple bond and its etiquette (adab) are held in the highest regard.
  • Watchful meditation (muraqaba): quietly observing one's own heart and staying alert to passing thoughts, an inner discipline parallel to remembrance.
  • Saints and their shrines: love for departed saints (wali, "the friend/nearest to God") is expressed through visiting their tombs (ziyara) and gathering on the anniversary of their death (urs) to seek blessing. This layer is especially strong in South Asia and Africa—and is exactly where revivalists raise their sharpest objections.

It bears emphasizing: all of this is added on top of the ordinary Muslim's Five Pillars, not a replacement for them. Most Sufis keep the five daily prayers, fast in Ramadan, and make the pilgrimage as usual; Sufi practice is "an inner discipline added within the shell," not a new enterprise built apart from it.

Common Misreadings

Misreading: Sufism is a "liberal," anti-legal departure from Islam.
Historically most Sufis kept the Law strictly. Al-Ghazali, who brought Sufism into the mainstream, was a leading jurist; Ibn Arabi and Rumi were devout Muslims. Sufism is the inner dimension of Islam, not a substitute for it.

Misreading: the whirling "dervish" is the universal image of a Sufi.
Whirling is the specific ritual of one order, the Mevlevi—a bodily form of dhikr, not a performance, and by no means shared by all Sufis; many orders practice only silent remembrance.

Misreading: Sufi = the politically "moderate, peaceful" good Muslim.
The West often romanticizes Sufism as an anti-extremist label. But Sufi orders have historically organized resistance and holy war (as in the Caucasus, North Africa, and West Africa's anti-colonial movements). Using it for present-day political sorting oversimplifies a complex tradition.

Misreading: Rumi is a "universal spiritual poet unrelated to religion."
Rumi was a devout Muslim scholar, and the Masnavi is saturated with Qur'anic and prophetic allusion. Western translations that strip away its Islamic context are a re-creation, not the original.

Across Traditions · The Contemporary Situation

A family resemblance among mysticisms. "Purify the self, dissolve into the divine, take love as the road"—this structure is not unique to Islam. Jewish Kabbalah, Christian contemplation (as when John of the Cross wrote of "the dark night of the soul"), and Hindu devotion (bhakti) all describe a kindred path. The mystical strands of the great traditions often resemble one another more than they resemble the legal mainstream of their own tradition. (Day 22, "Mysticism, Shared and Not," takes up this thread directly.)

Experience vs. mediation, a universal tension. Wherever there is a pursuit of "directly realizing God," it tends to chafe against a mainstream that mediates through institution and text—al-Hallaj to the Law is as certain mystics are to the Church. The wisdom of the sober Sufis is to fold the peak experience back into the Law and into daily life.

Caught between two pressures today. On one side, revivalists condemn its shrine-veneration as heresy, sometimes resorting to violence; on the other, the West romanticizes it and consumes it out of context. Sufism sits precisely between these two forces, and for that reason is often seen as an internal resource against extremism. To understand it requires seeing this "discipline of love" without either idealizing or belittling it.

Going Deeper

Why does mysticism, which stresses "firsthand experience," tend to chafe against the "keep the Law, keep the doctrine" mainstream in nearly every religion?
Because the two give different answers to "where does authority come from." The mainstream locates it in something publicly checkable and external—scripture, the Law, lineage, consensus; mysticism appeals to an inner experience that cannot be shared or verified. When someone says "I have realized the Truth," that can neither be reviewed by others nor prevented from being used to override norms, so the mainstream instinctively guards against it. Conversely, if only the shell remains without the inner, faith easily hollows out. A healthy tradition usually needs the two to calibrate each other: experience infuses the Law with life, and the Law sets a boundary for experience. The dispute between the sober and intoxicated schools within Sufism is exactly this calibration line, tugged back and forth from within.
If "self-annihilation" runs all the way to al-Hallaj's "I am the Truth," why is it dangerous? What separates the sober from the intoxicated school?
In strict monotheism there is an uncrossable line between Creator and creature. In the summit of self-loss, the intoxicated blurt out words of union with God ("I am the Truth")—true, perhaps, as experience, but in doctrinal terms it looks as if that line has been flattened, even as if one were claiming to be God, which is precisely why al-Hallaj was condemned. The sober school (like al-Junayd) does not deny such experience, but holds that real maturity is not to remain in intoxication, but to return, sober, from union, and to keep the Law and one's daily duties again. The difference is not whether one has reached the summit, but how one places one's words and deeds after it—which also explains why the "sober Sufism" of al-Ghazali's kind is the strand the mainstream ultimately embraced.
Why did Sufism take its deepest root precisely in places like West Africa and South Asia?
Largely because Sufism was the most supple vehicle for Islam as it spread into new regions. Centered on the sheikh and networked through shrines, the orders could dovetail with local customs of saint-reverence, song, and community, letting people enter Islam without a total break from their old life; forms like qawwali and shrine anniversaries carry a strong local affinity of their own. So in West Africa the Tijaniyya and Mouridiyya became almost the backbone of society and economy; in South Asia the Chishti shrines remain cross-faith centers of pilgrimage to this day. The contrast is with currents that stress purity and oppose shrines and localization—their tension with Sufism is, in a sense, a contest between two logics of spreading a faith: "how to take local root" versus "how to stay pure."

This piece introduces Sufism from a neutral, comparative standpoint, aiming to present its internal logic and diversity without praise, blame, or ranking, and without judging any living religious figure. Figures and historical details follow common scholarly accounts, and dates are often traditional or estimated; the size of the following varies enormously depending on whether one counts "formal initiation into an order" or "broad influence," and is therefore given as a range.
