World Religions

Hinduism

Day 9Dharmic tradition~1.2 billion adherents

Hinduism breaks almost every assumption packed into the word "religion": it has no founder, no single scripture, no central church, no one creed you must sign — yet it is the world's oldest living major religion and its third largest. Less a single "religion" than an entire family of traditions grown from South Asian soil over three thousand years, its unity lies not in doctrine but in a shared vocabulary: karma, rebirth, liberation, dharma.

"Truth is one; the wise call it by many names."— Rigveda 1.164.46 (Ekaṃ sat viprā bahudhā vadanti), often cited as the epigraph of Hinduism's "one reality, many paths" spirit

Origins & History

A religion with no beginning

Hindus often call their own tradition sanatana dharma — "the eternal law" — meaning it has no start and no founder. The word "Hinduism" itself is quite recent: it grew from a Persian, then European, umbrella term for "the beliefs of those living east of the Indus (Sindhu / Hindu)," and was only fixed as the name of "a religion" in the colonial 19th century. In other words, it was an outsider's classifying gaze that bundled a plural, overlapping, borderless mass of practice into "one Hinduism."

The traceable outline runs roughly thus. The Vedic period (c. 1500–500 BCE): the four Vedas, beginning with the Rigveda, take shape — a sacrificial religion of fire ritual, hymns, and liturgical precision. The Upanishads (c. 700–500 BCE) turn the gaze from the outer altar inward, asking after the ultimate reality of the cosmos (Brahman) and the essence of the self (Atman), and laying the substrate for all later Indian thought.

Then comes the epic and classical synthesis: the two great epics, the Mahabharata and Ramayana — with the Bhagavad Gita embedded in the former — and the many Puranas fuse abstract Brahman with living deities, temple worship, and distinct sects. The Vishnu, Shiva, and Goddess traditions familiar today largely crystallize here. The medieval Bhakti (devotion) movement brings another wave: bypassing elaborate ritual and priestly mediation, it holds that pure love and surrender to a personal deity (above all Krishna and Rama) reaches God directly — often with an anti-hierarchical edge that deeply shaped popular faith.

In the modern era, through colonialism, reform (the reinterpretations of figures such as Ram Mohan Roy and Vivekananda) and independence, Hinduism has both gone global (the worldwide spread of yoga and spirituality) and been drawn into modern identity politics.

Distribution Today

A civilization's religion, and a diasporic web

There are roughly 1.2 billion Hindus worldwide (recent estimates range from about 1.15 to 1.2 billion), around 15% of humanity — the third-largest organized religion after Christianity and Islam. The distribution is extraordinarily concentrated in South Asia:

  • India: about 1.1 billion, roughly 79% of the country, and home to some 94% of the world's Hindus.
  • Nepal: about 24 million, around 81% of the population — the highest Hindu proportion of any country.
  • Then minorities in Bangladesh, Pakistan, Indonesia (notably Bali), Sri Lanka, and others; in Mauritius Hindus are close to half the population.
  • Colonial-era indentured labor carried it to the Caribbean (Guyana, Trinidad, Suriname), Fiji, South and East Africa; recent migration has built new communities in the United States, United Kingdom, and Canada.

The key point: Hinduism is essentially an "ethnic–civilizational" religion, deeply entangled with the land and culture of India, and historically rarely proselytizing — it has spread more by migration than by mission. This sets it sharply apart from the universalizing, missionary character of Christianity and Islam, and explains why, vast as it is, its center of gravity remains overwhelmingly South Asian.

Core Belief System

One reality, countless manifestations

Beneath the profusion of deities and sects, most Hindu traditions share a core set of ideas:

  • Brahman and Atman: Brahman is the ultimate reality of the cosmos, all-pervading and formless; Atman is the innermost true self of each living being. The startling claim of the Upanishads is "you are that" (tat tvam asi) — the true self and ultimate reality are one, and to see through to this is liberation.
  • Karma and samsara: karma means "action and its consequence" — not fate but a moral law of cause and effect that drives life to cycle endlessly through birth and death (samsara). Your present condition is the fruit of past action; your present action writes the future.
  • Moksha (liberation): the ultimate goal is not "going to heaven" but stepping out of the cycle itself — total freedom from birth and death. This differs fundamentally from the Abrahamic frame of "one life, then judgment."
  • Dharma: the "law / duty / right order" that sustains cosmos and society, and each person's proper obligation within it (detailed in Day 10).

As for the perennial question of "how many gods": the internal spectrum is extremely wide — from a monism that sees Brahman as the sole impersonal reality, to a near-monotheism devoted to one supreme personal God, to a polytheistic practice acknowledging countless deities — often coexisting in the same individual. A common way to hold it: the many gods are different faces of, and doorways into, one and the same reality.

Principles & Practice

At home, and before the image

Hindu practice centers not on a weekly congregational service but on daily domestic life and personal devotion. Core forms include:

  • Puja and darshan: at a home shrine or temple, offering lamp, flowers, incense and food before an image (murti) of the deity. The image is held to be a vessel of divine presence, and darshan — to see the deity and be seen by it — is itself a grace.
  • Three paths: Hinduism grants that many roads lead to liberation. The Bhagavad Gita distills them into karma yoga (the way of selfless action), bhakti yoga (the way of love and devotion), and jnana yoga (the way of wisdom and meditation). One chooses a path by temperament — the practical face of its "many paths" spirit.
  • Mantra and meditation: chanting sacred sounds such as "Om" and other mantras, along with the many forms of yoga and meditation, to gather the mind and draw near the sacred.
  • Festivals and pilgrimage: Diwali (the victory of light over dark), Holi (spring and revelry), Navaratri (the nine nights of the Goddess), and the Kumbh Mela, where tens of millions gather — reckoned among the largest religious assemblies on Earth.
  • Ahimsa (non-harm) and vegetarianism: the ethic of not harming living beings leads many (though not all) Hindus to vegetarianism; the cow, as a gentle, nourishing symbol, is honored and protected.
  • Life-cycle rites (samskara): from naming and first learning to marriage and cremation, a sequence of rituals marks the stages of a life.

An important caveat: nearly every item above varies by region, sect, and family. There is no single "correct practice" — a Tamil Shaiva and a North Indian Krishna devotee may practice very differently while belonging to the same tradition. The social dimensions — caste, the life-stages (ashrama), and dharma as duty — are reserved for Day 10.

Common Misreadings

Misreading: Hinduism is simply a polytheism worshipping thousands of gods.
Its internal spectrum runs from monism to near-monotheism to polytheistic practice. The mainstream reading is that the many gods are largely different manifestations of, and doorways into, one ultimate reality (Brahman) — not independent, separate deities.

Misreading: the caste system is Hinduism's core doctrine.
Caste is a complex social-historical phenomenon whose relationship to religion is itself hotly contested within the tradition. The Bhakti movement, modern reformers, and many contemporary Hindus fiercely criticize caste discrimination; India's constitution bans it. It is not one unified doctrine that must be believed.

Misreading: Hindus "worship cows."
The cow is protected out of the ethic of non-harm and its symbolism of nourishment and gentleness — a matter of reverence, not of treating the cow as a god.

Misreading: karma means "predestined fate."
Karma literally means "action." It describes a law of cause and consequence and in fact stresses responsibility for present choices — the opposite of a fatalism that leaves everything to destiny.

Cross-Tradition Reading · Contemporary Situation

The shared bedrock of the Dharmic family. The vocabulary of karma, rebirth, and liberation is the ground Hinduism shares with Jainism and Sikhism (Days 11–12), as well as with Buddhism — different branches from the same soil, diverging over whether the self truly exists and how liberation is reached. Grasp Hinduism, and those stations come much easier.

A deep contrast with the Abrahamic faiths. Linear time vs. cyclical time; one life under judgment vs. countless rebirths; one revealed God and one authoritative scripture vs. one reality in myriad manifestations and layered scriptures. This is not a question of better or worse, but of two different grammars for understanding time, self, and the ultimate.

Tensions today. The globalization of yoga and spirituality scatters Hindu elements across the world in a "de-religionized" guise; meanwhile "Hindu identity" is drawn into the politics of modern India (what scholars call Hindu nationalism, or Hindutva — a political ideology, not to be equated with Hinduism as a faith tradition); and internal reform over caste and gender is still under way.

Going Deeper

With no founder, no creed, and no central authority, on what grounds is this "one religion"?
This is a genuine scholarly question. Some argue that "Hinduism" is a construct bundled together only in the colonial era by an outsider's gaze — that before it there were only assorted local, overlapping practices. Others counter that, however varied the forms, most traditions do share a recognizable vocabulary (Brahman, karma, rebirth, liberation, dharma) and a textual lineage (the authority of the Vedas). Perhaps the more accurate account is that it is not a bounded "religion" but a family — members resemble one another and share ancestors, yet subscribe to no single doctrine everyone must sign. This is also why "who counts as a Hindu" remains an open and elastic question.
"The many gods are faces of one reality" — how does this hold monism, monotheism and polytheism at once?
The key is that Hinduism tends to think in levels rather than either/or. At the highest level, ultimate reality (Brahman) is one and formless; at the level of worship, a person needs an approachable face, hence personal deities and their avatars; in daily life, countless local deities have their spheres. The same individual may pray to Krishna at dawn, affirm that Brahman is the sole reality, and honor a village goddess at the local shrine — and see no contradiction, because these are understood as different heights of, and entrances to, one and the same reality. This "one-and-many" elasticity is what makes Hinduism hardest for outside frameworks to grasp — and among the most capacious.
How do cyclical time and rebirth reshape the meaning of a single life, and of suffering?
When life is not "once" but "countless times," the weight of a single lifetime is reweighed: it is neither the only chance nor the endpoint, but one stretch of a long journey. This can bring a certain equanimity (failure and suffering can be continued in a future life), but it also carries a risk — the logic that "this life's suffering stems from past-life karma," if abused, can rationalize present injustice (which is exactly what internal reformers attack). Against the urgency of the Abrahamic "one life, one judgment," this is a wholly different sense of time and moral gravity. Worth pressing: if the goal is to "step out of the cycle itself," is the "individual" ultimately saved within it, or transcended?
