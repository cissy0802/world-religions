Sunni & Shia

Day 7Abrahamic · IslamSunni ~85–90% · Shia ~10–13%

Islam's largest internal divide did not begin with a doctrine but with an unresolved question: after Muhammad's death, who was entitled to lead the community of believers? A counter-intuitive point: Sunnis and Shias share the same Qur'an, the same Prophet, the same testimony of faith, the same Five Pillars. What first separated them was a dispute over succession; only over the following centuries did it settle into distinct theology, law, and collective memory.

"This day I have perfected for you your religion."—— Qur'an 5:3, traditionally placed after Muhammad's farewell pilgrimage in 632; the question of "who comes next" is precisely where Sunni and Shia part ways

History & Origins

How a succession dispute grew into two traditions

When Muhammad died in 632 CE, he left no undisputed arrangement for a successor and no surviving son. Who would lead the ummah (community of believers)? Two answers emerged, later crystallizing into Sunni and Shia.

The majority held that leadership should fall to a worthy figure chosen by the community's consensus. Thus the Prophet's close companion Abu Bakr was acclaimed as the first caliph (khalifa, "successor"). Umar, Uthman, and Ali followed, later revered by Sunnis as the "Rightly Guided Caliphs" (Rashidun). The name Sunni derives from ahl al-sunna—"the people of the Prophet's practice and the community's consensus."

Another group believed the Prophet had designated his cousin and son-in-law Ali ibn Abi Talib as his heir (they place special weight on a declaration at Ghadir Khumm during the farewell journey), and that leadership should remain within the Prophet's family (ahl al-bayt, the household). Ali's partisans were called shi'at Ali—"the party of Ali," the origin of the name Shia. Ali did eventually become the fourth caliph, but was assassinated in 661 CE.

What forged the political rift into an irreversible religious identity was the Battle of Karbala in 680 CE. The Umayyad dynasty had made the caliphate hereditary; Ali's son and the Prophet's grandson Husayn refused to recognize its legitimacy and, with a few dozen followers, was surrounded and killed by an army of thousands at Karbala in present-day Iraq. Husayn's martyrdom became, for Shias, the supreme witness against unjust power, mourned each year on Ashura (the tenth day of Muharram in the Islamic calendar). Over the following centuries, both traditions developed mature systems of law and theology.

Distribution Today

Sunnis are the vast majority; Shias cluster in a few places

Among the world's Muslims, Sunnis make up roughly 85%–90% and Shias roughly 10%–13% (somewhere between 150 and 240 million, with ranges depending on the definition used). Sunnis are the mainstream in nearly every Muslim society from West Africa to Southeast Asia; Shias are more geographically concentrated:

  • Shia-majority: Iran (over 90%), Iraq (about 65%), Azerbaijan (a majority), Bahrain (a majority of its Muslim citizens). Iran is the demographic and political center of gravity for Shia Islam today.
  • Significant minorities: Lebanon (nearly half), Yemen (Zaydis), Pakistan and India (large minorities), and the Gulf coast including eastern Saudi Arabia.
  • In absolute numbers, most of the world's Shias live in four countries: Iran, Pakistan, India, and Iraq.

A point worth making plainly: Shia does not equal Persian, and Sunni does not equal Arab. Iran may be the Shia heartland, but there are many Shias among Arabs and South Asians alike; meanwhile the overwhelming majority of Muslims across the Arab world, South Asia, and Southeast Asia are Sunni. The two are geographically interwoven and have historically lived side by side far more often than in separated blocs—the antagonism seen in some regions in recent decades often overlays geopolitics and state rivalry rather than pure doctrinal conflict.

Core Belief System

The split is over "where authority comes from," not "who God is"

Both traditions affirm the oneness of God (tawhid), the Qur'an, Muhammad as the seal of the prophets, and resurrection and judgment—the skeleton of Islamic belief is shared. The real divide turns on one question: after the Prophet, who carries religious authority, and how is it transmitted?

The Sunni answer: consensus and learning

Sunni Islam has, in principle, no clerical hierarchy; the caliph is a political and communal leader, not an infallible religious intermediary. Religious authority derives from learning (the ulama, the scholars) and consensus (ijma), embodied in four schools of law—Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi'i, and Hanbali—which recognize one another and hold sway in different regions. The believer stands directly before God, without priestly mediation.

The Shia answer: the Imam

Shias believe that religious and spiritual leadership after the Prophet belongs to a divinely chosen Imam drawn from the household—not merely a political leader but the authoritative interpreter of scripture, divinely protected from error. The mainstream Twelvers hold that there were twelve Imams, the last of whom entered "occultation" (ghayba) in the ninth century and will return at the end of time as the Mahdi to establish justice. Other Shia branches include the Ismailis and the Zaydis. Their school of law is the Ja'fari school (named for the sixth Imam, Ja'far al-Sadiq).

Principles & Practice

Shared Five Pillars, differing details and memory

In everyday devotion, both traditions keep the testimony of faith, prayer, almsgiving, fasting, and pilgrimage—a Sunni and a Shia circling the Kaaba in Mecca do so with much the same motions. The differences lie in details, legal sources, and collective memory:

  • Details of prayer: Shias often combine the five daily prayers into three time-slots, and may place a small tablet of earth (frequently from Karbala) before the forehead in prostration; the exact rites vary by school.
  • Veneration of the household and shrine visitation: Shias hold Ali, Fatima, Husayn and the household in special reverence, and beyond the Mecca pilgrimage they undertake visitation (ziyara) to the shrines of the Imams at Najaf, Karbala, Mashhad, and Qom.
  • Mourning at Ashura: in the month of Muharram, Shias commemorate Husayn's martyrdom through recitation, mourning, and processions—the most vivid public expression of Shia identity.
  • Structure of religious authority: Shias developed a layered scholarly hierarchy in which believers may choose to follow a living senior scholar (marja', a "source of emulation"—the kind of figure often called an ayatollah); Sunnis, by contrast, turn on the schools of law and scholarly consensus, without such an institutional hierarchy.
  • Points of law: rules on inheritance, temporary marriage (mut'a, recognized among Twelvers), and religious taxes (Shias also levy khums, "the fifth") differ between the two.

Yet it bears emphasizing: these differences sit atop a vast shared foundation. Across most periods and places, Sunnis and Shias have lived as neighbors, intermarried, and shared markets. To imagine them as two implacably opposed religions badly oversimplifies the internal picture of Islam.

Common Misreadings

Misreading: Sunni and Shia are two different religions.
Both belong to Islam and share the Qur'an, the Prophet, the testimony of faith, and the Five Pillars. The divide arose over leadership after the Prophet—a difference of tradition within Islam, not a difference of religions.

Misreading: Shias "worship" Ali or the Imams.
Shias hold strictly to the oneness of God and worship God alone. They revere, commemorate, and visit the shrines of the household and the Imams as exemplars and intercessory objects of love—which is fundamentally different from worship, just as revering the Prophet is not worshipping him.

Misreading: Sunnis and Shias have been at war since the beginning.
Historically the two have mostly lived side by side and intermarried. The antagonism seen in some regions today usually overlays state rivalry, geopolitics, and identity mobilization; reducing it to a fixed 1,400-year "sectarian blood feud" is inaccurate.

Misreading: Shia means Iranian/Persian.
Shias are found across the Arab world (Iraq, Lebanon, the Gulf) and South Asia, and are mostly of Arab or South Asian ethnicity; Iran is simply the single country where they are most numerous and influential. Ethnicity and sect do not map onto each other.

Across Traditions · The Contemporary Situation

A universal problem of "succession." After a founder's death, how is authority continued? This is not a dilemma unique to Islam. Christianity, after the apostles, appealed to "apostolic succession" and the episcopate (Days 3 and 4); Judaism, after the Temple, turned to rabbis and the oral law (Day 1). Sunnis hand the answer to learning and consensus, Shias to a designated line—a contrast worth setting beside the Catholic/Orthodox "succession" and the Protestant "scripture alone": does authority rest on a human lineage, or on text and consensus? Each great tradition offers a different solution.

The power of martyr-memory. The Shia tradition of mourning built around Husayn's martyrdom invites comparison with the Christian narrative of the Passion—the sacrifice of one killed by unjust power becoming a moral compass and identity-core for an entire community. Scholars of comparative religion often use this to discuss how "martyrdom" shapes collective memory.

Tensions today. The contemporary Sunni–Shia relationship is often swept up in the state rivalries and geopolitical contests of the Middle East; understanding this divide requires keeping seventh-century theological history distinct from twenty-first-century political reality—related, but not identical. The next installment (Day 8) turns to Sufism, the mystical tradition that runs across both.

Going Deeper

Why would a "political succession problem" ultimately become a "theological divide"?
The original dispute was indeed largely political: who should lead the community. But the question of a leader's legitimacy quickly pulled in deeper theological questions—must a leader possess special, divinely protected qualities? Can religious authority be separated from political power? Who has the right to interpret scripture definitively? The Shia answers (the Imam is divinely protected, scripture requires interpretation by the household) differ fundamentally from the Sunni answers (authority comes from learning and community consensus). So "who should lead" gradually evolved into an entire theology of authority, infallibility, and salvation. Karbala then injected an emotional core of martyrdom and justice, turning what had been a partisan stance into an irreversible religious identity.
With no church and no line of Imams, who defines Sunni "orthodoxy"?
Sunni Islam lacks a central authority that can settle matters once and for all—both a flexibility and a difficulty. Traditionally, orthodoxy is defined gradually by the community of scholars (ulama) through long scholarly transmission, the master-to-student chains within each school of law, and cross-school consensus (ijma)—there is no pope-like arbiter, only the accumulation of scholarly prestige and communal acceptance. This yields considerable pluralism and local flexibility, but it also means that in the modern era, as traditional scholarly authority is weakened while anyone can broadcast a view through media, "who is entitled to speak for Sunni orthodoxy" has become a real and open contest—a key to understanding several present-day currents of dispute.
If the two share so much foundation, why does the divide remain so deep?
Because the divide lies not only in propositions but in memory and identity. Sunnis and Shias overlap heavily on the skeleton of doctrine, but each has accumulated 1,400 years of collective memory and emotion around different historical events (the Rightly Guided Caliphs vs. Karbala), different sacred figures, and different rituals and observances. Identity is often sustained by these living memories rather than by abstract dogma. For that very reason, reconciliation and conflict between the two depend more on present-day social and political conditions than on any single doctrinal difference that could be "resolved" by debate; treating it as a purely theoretical problem both underestimates the power of memory and misjudges the true source of the tension.

This piece introduces the Sunni and Shia traditions from a neutral, comparative standpoint, aiming to present the internal logic of each without praise, blame, or taking sides, and without judging any living religious figure. Names and historical details follow common scholarly accounts; dates are often traditional or estimated; population figures are recent estimates and are given as ranges because definitions and methods differ.
