World Religions

Judaism

Day 1Abrahamic tradition~15 million adherents

To understand Christianity and Islam, you first have to understand Judaism — the shared root of all three Abrahamic faiths. It is also one of the oldest continuous monotheisms: a people that has never exceeded 0.2% of the world's population, yet wrote ideas like "one God," "history has a direction," and "humans bear moral responsibility for the world" into the operating system of half the planet.

"Hear, O Israel: the Lord our God, the Lord is One."— The Shema, Deuteronomy 6:4, the central declaration of Jewish faith

Origins & History

From a covenant to a people

Judaism's story begins with the covenant of Abraham: traditionally around 1800 BCE, Abraham enters into a covenant with God, the starting point of monotheistic faith. Then comes Moses and the Exodus — at Mount Sinai the people receive the Torah (literally "teaching / instruction"), and the covenant expands from a family to a whole nation. (The historicity of the Exodus remains debated archaeologically, but its status as the founding narrative is irreplaceable.)

What follows is a history of "Temple and dispersion" cycles. Around the 10th century BCE, Solomon builds the First Temple in Jerusalem; in 586 BCE the Babylonians destroy it and deport the elite — the "Babylonian exile." The rebuilt Second Temple is destroyed by the Romans in 70 CE — a decisive moment: with the Temple and its sacrificial center gone, Judaism is forced to reinvent itself.

Rabbis (teachers) replace priests; the synagogue replaces the Temple, and study and prayer replace sacrifice. The oral law is written down as the Mishnah (c. 200 CE) and the Talmud (c. 500 CE) — a vast compendium of debate still studied page by page today. It is precisely this "rabbinic Judaism" that let a landless people endure for nearly two thousand years.

The Middle Ages split Jewry into two great branches, Ashkenazi (Central/Eastern Europe) and Sephardi (Spain, the Mediterranean); the expulsion from Spain came in 1492. The modern era brought the Enlightenment (Haskalah), anti-Jewish violence (pogroms), and finally the Holocaust — some six million Jews murdered by the Nazis, European Jewish civilization nearly torn out by the roots. In 1948 the State of Israel is founded, giving the two-millennia-old prayer "next year in Jerusalem" a concrete anchor — and opening new tensions.

Distribution Today

A small, concentrated world people

The global Jewish population is roughly 15 million (14.7–15.7 million depending on definition), about 0.2% of the world — among the smallest of the major religions. It is highly concentrated at two poles:

  • Israel: about 7.1 million, the world's only Jewish-majority state.
  • United States: about 6–7.5 million (by definition), the largest diaspora community, concentrated in New York, California, Florida.
  • Then France, Canada, the UK, Argentina, Russia, Germany, Australia, each in the hundreds of thousands.

The key point: Jewish identity is doubly "ethnic-and-religious" (an ethnoreligion). One can be a "secular Jew" — Jewish by culture and descent, yet not religiously observant. Traditionally Jewish identity passes through the maternal line (Reform also accepts patrilineal descent); conversion is possible, but Judaism does not actively proselytize and may even discourage converts at first — a sharp contrast with Christianity and Islam, and part of why its numbers have always stayed small.

Core Beliefs

Deed over creed, this world over the next

One God and covenant

Judaism is strict monotheism: God is one, formless, indivisible (precisely what the Shema declares). The core is not "which doctrines you believe" but covenant (brit) — God chooses the people of Israel, the two enter a pact, and the people respond to God's grace by keeping the commandments.

The real meaning of "chosen"

"Chosen people" is often misread as superiority; it in fact means responsibility, not privilege: chosen to carry the Torah, to be "a light unto the nations." Tradition even says God offered the Torah to every nation, and only Israel accepted the burden.

Three key divergences from Christianity

  • No original sin: human nature has two inclinations, toward good and toward evil (yetzer), guided by action and law — not a fallen nature requiring salvation.
  • The Messiah has not yet come: Judaism still awaits the Messiah (mashiach) — a human king who will bring worldly peace and justice, not God. This is the most fundamental divergence from Christianity.
  • This-worldly focus: doctrine says little about the afterlife; the focus is on how to live a holy, just life in this world.
Principles & Practice

Weaving the sacred into the everyday

Jewish spirituality lies not in solitary contemplation but in sanctifying every part of life through countless concrete acts (mitzvot, commandments). Tradition counts 613 commandments. Core practices include:

  • Shabbat (the Sabbath): from Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall, all "work" ceases — no labor, no fire or electricity, shared meals and prayer with family. A weekly exercise in "you are not a machine," widely regarded as one of Judaism's deepest gifts to the world.
  • Kashrut (dietary law): distinguishing clean from unclean foods (no pork or shellfish, meat and dairy kept separate), turning the most everyday act — eating — into ongoing mindful practice.
  • Life-cycle rites: circumcision of male infants (brit milah) as the sign of the covenant; the bar/bat mitzvah at 13, taking on the responsibility of the commandments.
  • Prayer and study: three daily prayers; studying Torah and Talmud is itself worship — Judaism treats questioning and debate as forms of devotion.
  • Festivals: Rosh Hashanah (New Year), Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement), Passover, Sukkot, Shavuot, Hanukkah, Purim — each re-enacting a piece of collective memory.
  • Tikkun Olam ("repairing the world") and Tzedakah: the duty to mend the world, and "doing justice" (tzedakah means both charity and justice) — grounding ethics in social action.

The three major movements today

  • Orthodox: the Torah is divinely revealed and the law fully binding; internally divided into Modern Orthodox and Haredi (ultra-Orthodox).
  • Conservative (Masorti): keeps tradition but accepts that law can be interpreted and evolve with the times.
  • Reform: emphasizes ethical spirit over ritual detail, the earliest to embrace gender equality and modern life.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "chosen" means Jews consider themselves superior.
It means responsibility, not superiority — chosen to keep the law and carry the moral burden. Traditional texts stress repeatedly that this is a weight, not a reward.

Misconception: Judaism is just "Old Testament Christianity."
Their theologies differ greatly: Judaism has no original sin or Trinity, the Messiah has not yet come, and its living tradition is the rabbinic Talmud, not the Christian New Testament.

Misconception: all Jews are religiously observant.
Jewish identity is both ethnic and religious. Both Israel and the US have large numbers of "secular Jews" who identify culturally but do not practice.

Misconception: Judaism equals Zionism.
The former is a religious tradition; the latter a political-national movement that arose in the 19th century. They are related but not identical, and Jews hold extremely diverse, often conflicting, views on Israeli policy.

Cross-Tradition Bridge · Contemporary Standing

Three faiths, one source. Judaism is the matrix of Christianity and Islam: Jesus was a Jew, and the Qur'an recognizes Abraham and Moses as prophets. The three share a skeleton of one God, prophets, covenant, and eschatology, yet part ways over "has the Messiah come?" and "did God become incarnate?" Grasp Judaism and the next issues (Day 2–8) go much more smoothly.

Underrated intellectual export. "History has a direction rather than cycles," "redemption will come within time," "humans bear moral responsibility for society" — these premises seep into Western law, ethics, and even secular ideas of progress, and their source often lies here.

Tensions today. The trauma of Holocaust memory, the complex relationship between Israel and the diaspora, resurgent antisemitism, debates over secularization and intermarriage — how Judaism holds onto itself between "continuity" and "adaptation" is a question two thousand years old and still unfinished.

For Reflection

How did a people with no land and no central authority survive for nearly two thousand years?
The key may be that Judaism turned the physical center — the Temple — into something portable: a book (the Torah), a system of time (Shabbat and the festivals), a way of life carried anywhere (kashrut, prayer, study). When the sacred depends not on a place but on every family's weekly repetition, it becomes nearly impossible to destroy. For anyone trying to understand "how a culture resists extinction," this is a powerful case — especially set against other ancient peoples who vanished once they lost their homeland.
How does a "this-worldly, afterlife-light" faith answer suffering and injustice?
When the focus is not "reward after death" but "doing justice in this world," suffering cannot be brushed off with "compensation in the next life." This is both a burden and a strength: it fuels a strong tradition of social justice (tikkun olam, the prophets' fury at oppression), and it makes theodicy (why a just God permits the Holocaust) an especially sharp, unavoidable problem in Jewish thought. Contrast the Yom Kippur practice of repentance — collective and repair-oriented, rather than individual and salvation-oriented.
Treating questioning and debate as devotion — how does this differ from most religions' emphasis on submission?
The Talmud itself is a record of debate that preserves minority opinions and invites line-by-line challenge. In Jewish tradition, "arguing" with God (as Abraham bargains for Sodom, or Job questions God) is not seen as irreverent but as a mark of intimacy. This contrasts intriguingly with faith orientations that stress unconditional submission, and may partly explain the vitality of intellectual argument in Jewish culture. The question worth pressing: where does this "sacred argument" stop, and who sets the boundary?
