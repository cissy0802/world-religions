The Hindu Life

Day 10Indian traditions~1.2 billion Hindus

Day 9 covered what Hindus believe; this one covers how they live. For most Hindus, religion's center of gravity is not creed but dharma—the duty that follows from one's position. A life is located by three coordinates: which group you are born into (caste), which stage of life you have reached, and which of life's aims you pursue. Grasp this grid, and you have stepped into the Hindu everyday.

"Better one's own duty done imperfectly than another's duty done well."—— Bhagavad Gita 3:35, the classic statement of svadharma (one's own dharma)

History & Origins

How a social order was sanctified—and challenged

The social skeleton of Hindu life traces back to the oldest text, the Rig Veda. One hymn, the "Purusha Sukta," describes in mythic terms how the four varnas (varna, literally "color/class") emerged from the body of a primordial giant: the Brahmin (priest) from the mouth, the Kshatriya (warrior-ruler) from the arms, the Vaishya (farmer-merchant) from the thighs, the Shudra (laborer) from the feet. This fourfold frame was later systematized into obligation in the Dharmashastra texts such as the Manusmriti (compiled from roughly the 2nd century BCE to the 3rd century CE).

But the key point: varna is a theory of four categories, while lived society runs on jati—thousands of concrete communities defined by hereditary occupation and region. The two are often lumped together as "caste," yet they are two distinct layers: one an abstract scriptural schema, the other a teeming, mobile social reality.

This order was never beyond challenge. The medieval bhakti (devotional) movements repeatedly proclaimed all souls equal before God; Sikhism, and later many reformers, critiqued caste hierarchy from within. In modern times Gandhi called the "untouchables" Harijan ("children of God") and pushed to abolish untouchability, while B. R. Ambedkar—himself born into an "untouchable" community and later chief drafter of the Constitution—went further, judging caste too deeply entangled with Hindu doctrine, and late in life led a mass conversion to Buddhism. Article 17 of the Indian Constitution, effective 1950, formally abolished "untouchability" and made it a punishable offense, and set up reservations (quotas) in education and public office for historically oppressed groups.

Distribution Today

The law is repealed, society is not: a net not yet gone

Of today's roughly 1.2 billion Hindus, the vast majority live in India (about 80% of its population). Caste is legally void, yet persists at varying intensity in marriage, residence, and social ties, with wide urban-rural differences. A few frequently cited figures (all census/large-survey ranges, differing by definition):

  • Scheduled Castes (SC, the historically "untouchable" groups): about 201 million per the 2011 Indian census, roughly 16.6% of India's population; the true figure is higher, as unregistered Christian and Muslim Dalits are not counted.
  • Other Backward Classes (OBC): various estimates put them at roughly 40% of India's population (no unified official census figure; the range is disputed).
  • Inter-caste marriage: surveys still show it as a minority, on the order of 5%–10%—a direct index of how caste endures in the private sphere.

Worth spelling out: Hinduism is an "ethnic-civilizational" rather than a "universalizing, missionary" tradition, and caste/jati belonging has historically been nearly coextensive with being born Hindu—not freely chosen the way one might convert. This makes "Dalit rights" and "reservations" among the most enduring issues in Indian politics; in diaspora Indian communities, debates over whether to write caste discrimination into anti-discrimination law (as at some U.S. institutions) are echoes of the same net.

Core Belief System

Four aims of life, one path of duty

The "meaning framework" of a Hindu life can be grasped through two sets of classic concepts. The first is the four aims of life (purushartha)—which bring worldly pursuits and ultimate liberation onto legitimate footing side by side, rather than pitting them against each other:

The second set is what "locates" a person: varnashrama-dharma. Your duty is not an abstract universal rule but is concretely different according to your group (varna) and stage of life (ashrama). From this comes svadharma ("one's own duty")—the Gita's famous counsel: better to do your own duty imperfectly than another's duty well. This ties in with karma: one's present position is understood as the fruit of past action, and how one discharges that position's duties shapes the life to come.

To be stressed: this is a classical ideal schema, not a description of what everyone actually does. It explains why Hindu ethics is highly "situational"—the same act may be right or wrong depending on one's identity and stage of life.

Principles & Practice

From cradle to cremation: a life marked by rites

If Day 9 covered daily devotion before the home shrine, the other half of a Hindu life is a series of life-cycle rites that sanctify each stage of existence. Core practices include:

  • Sacraments (samskara): tradition lists about 16 rites spanning a lifetime—conception, naming, first feeding, the start of learning, the sacred-thread rite (for some castes), marriage, and on to the last rites and cremation. Each transforms a biological milestone into a religiously meaningful passage.
  • The householder at the center: among the four stages, the householder (grihastha)—who marries, raises children, hosts guests, and supports priests and renunciates—is seen as the pillar of society, sustaining the people in the other three stages. Most Hindus' lives are centered here.
  • Marriage and family: marriage is traditionally viewed as a family matter and a way of fulfilling dharma, so arranged matches long predominated (self-choice is now rising among urban youth); many families still seek partners within the same jati.
  • Purity and food: notions of the pure (shaucha) and impure permeate diet and daily life. Vegetarianism is the choice of a substantial portion, though not all, of Hindus—per a 2021 Pew survey, about 39% of Indian adults call themselves vegetarian and about 81% place some restriction on meat; the cow, as a symbol of gentle nurture, receives special protection.
  • Varying by place and group: as with Day 9's caveat—practice differs greatly by north/south, sect, jati, and town/country. Hinduism has no single "rulebook"; even "what counts as doing it right" is plural.

Common Misreadings

Misreading: caste is the core doctrine of Hindu faith, and all Hindus endorse it.
Caste is a socio-historical institution—entangled with doctrine but not identical to it. From medieval bhakti poets to modern reformers, critique of caste hierarchy from within Hinduism has never ceased; many believers today explicitly oppose caste discrimination.

Misreading: varna (the four categories) equals caste (jati).
Varna is the fourfold abstract schema of the texts; jati is the real-world reality of thousands of concrete groups by occupation and region. Equating them misreads the system's complexity and mobility.

Misreading: every Hindu renounces the world along the "four stages."
The four stages are an ideal model of life; only a minority actually pass through the forest-dweller and renunciate stages. For the great majority, life is centered on the "householder" stage—marrying, working, doing one's duty.

Misreading: caste was abolished, so it is long gone.
In law, the 1950 Constitution abolished untouchability and set up reservations; socially, it persists at varying intensity in marriage, residence, and personal ties. "Law changed, custom not yet" is precisely the central tension of ongoing Indian debate.

Cross-Tradition Reading · Contemporary Context

"Duty" is not unique to India. The ethical intuition that each should keep to their place and discharge their role echoes in the Confucian "let the ruler be a ruler, the subject a subject," in the three estates of medieval Europe, even in the Protestant "calling." Hinduism's distinctiveness is to bind it to karma, rebirth, and liberation in one vast cross-life explanatory system—which gives it great persuasive force, but also makes inequality harder to write off as mere chance.

Critique grown from within. Notably, the strongest challenges to caste came largely from within Indian traditions: Jainism, Sikhism, the bhakti movements, and Ambedkar's Dalit conversion to Buddhism (Days 11–12 will expand on these). A reminder that a tradition's "self-expression" and its "self-critique" often share a source.

Tensions today. Debates over the fairness of reservations (affirmative action), social resistance to inter-caste marriage, Dalit rights movements, and disputes over anti-caste-discrimination legislation in the Indian diaspora—how this ancient coordinate grid of "dharma" coexists with modern equality and individual freedom is a question still being written, far from settled.

Going Deeper

"Better your own duty done imperfectly than another's done well"—wisdom, or a cage?
Both readings hold. On the favorable side, it is an anti-anxiety steadiness: it turns attention from "comparing with others" to "doing your own part well," akin to the Gita's teaching to act without clinging to results—grounding and focus. On the other side, when "your duty" is bound to a hereditary status, it can also become a rationale for freezing inequality and urging people to accept their lot. The crux may be: is svadharma an inner calling (the duty I claim) or an external assignment (the slot society hands me)? For two centuries, Hindu reformers have argued over exactly this distinction.
Does explaining a person's present lot by "karma" slide into "the sufferer had it coming"?
This is karma's sharpest ethical difficulty. Karma can indeed be invoked to say "the present has its prior causes," dissolving the question of injustice—critics call this fatalism. But the tradition also answers back: karma stresses that present action shapes the future, and so is a summons to act, not resignation; and to excuse another's suffering by karma while withholding help is itself bad karma. Ambedkar and others held that once karma is used to justify caste oppression, it must be critiqued or even discarded. The same doctrine can license opposite ethical stances—the interpreter's choice is decisive.
Why does a tradition that honors "worldly householding" as life's center also set "renunciation" as its highest goal?
The apparent contradiction is resolved precisely by the four stages: rather than force a choice, they sequence the two—first engage the world fully as a householder, discharging duties to family and society, and only later turn toward liberation. Worldly wealth and desire are not condemned as sin but seen as legitimate aims, each with its season. So "pleasure" and "liberation" need not oppose each other; they are earlier and later stretches of one curve. This makes an interesting contrast with traditions that treat worldly desire itself as a fall—and is one key to why Hinduism can hold both worldly splendor and extreme asceticism at once.

This piece introduces the Hindu life and social order from a neutral, comparative stance, aiming to present at once its internal logic, its internal critique, and its contemporary controversies, without praise, blame, or ranking. On caste and related matters it sets the tradition's own self-expression alongside the inequality and reform found in history, endorsing no side.
