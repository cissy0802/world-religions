World Religions

The Birth of Islam

Day 6Abrahamic tradition~1.9 billion adherents

After Judaism and Christianity, the third Abrahamic faith arrives in seventh-century Arabia. It does not see itself as a "new" religion but as a return to the original monotheism of Adam, Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. Here is the counterintuitive part: Muslims do not believe Islam began with Muhammad. They hold that he was only the last — the seal — of the prophets, sent to state one final time a message people had always forgotten.

"There is no god but God; Muhammad is the messenger of God."— the Shahada, Islam's core declaration of faith; to recite it is to become a Muslim

Origins & History

From a cave on Mount Hira to a faith across three continents

Around 570 CE, Muhammad was born in Mecca, a trading city in the Arabian Peninsula. Polytheism prevailed there; the Kaaba, a cube-shaped shrine in Mecca, housed idols of many tribal gods. Tradition holds that around 610 CE, while meditating in a cave on Mount Hira at the age of forty, Muhammad received his first revelation: the angel Jibril (the Gabriel of the Bible) commanded him to "Recite." Over the next twenty-odd years he received words believed by the faithful to be the verbatim revelation of God (Arabic Allah, "the one God"), gathered into the Qur'an (meaning "recitation").

His core message was strikingly simple: there is only one God, and human beings should submit to Him — "Islam" literally means "submission," and "Muslim" means "one who submits." This message enraged the Meccan elite, whose livelihood depended on the polytheistic shrine. Under persecution, Muhammad and his followers migrated in 622 CE to the northern city of Yathrib (later called Medina). This migration, the Hijra, marks year one of the Islamic calendar and the beginning of a faith community (the ummah) as a political as well as religious body.

In Medina, Muhammad was both prophet and community leader — making covenants, judging disputes, defending the city. In 630 CE he entered Mecca peacefully, cleared the idols from the Kaaba, and rededicated it to the one God. By his death in 632 CE, most of Arabia had come under monotheism. Within the following century, under his successors (the caliphs), Muslim rule expanded rapidly — west to the Iberian Peninsula, east to Central Asia and the Indus valley — carrying not only armies but a civilization that would shine for centuries in mathematics, medicine, philosophy, and astronomy.

Distribution Today

Not a Middle Eastern religion, but an Asian one

There are roughly 1.9 billion Muslims worldwide (recent estimates range about 1.85–2.0 billion), about a quarter of humanity — the second-largest religion after Christianity, and the fastest-growing of the past decade. A commonly misread fact: the demographic center of Islam lies in South and Southeast Asia, not the Arab world.

  • Indonesia: about 240 million, the country with the most Muslims on earth — and not in the Middle East.
  • Pakistan: about 230 million; India about 200 million (a minority, yet one of the largest Muslim populations anywhere); Bangladesh about 150 million.
  • The Arabic-speaking world (the Middle East and North Africa) together accounts for only about a fifth of all Muslims; sub-Saharan Africa is one of the fastest-growing regions.

Islam is a classic universal, missionary faith: regardless of race or lineage, anyone who professes the Shahada is a Muslim — a sharp contrast with Judaism's "ethnoreligion." Internally, about 85%–90% are Sunni and about 10%–13% are Shia (mainly in Iran and Iraq), with Sufi devotional traditions running across both — divisions that are the subject of Day 7 and Day 8.

Core Beliefs

The oneness of God, lived by Book and example

Tawhid — the oneness of God

The absolute center of Islamic theology is tawhid: the oneness and indivisibility of God. God is one, formless, without image, without partner or son, beyond all created things. For this reason Islam forbids all idols and images of the divine, and rejects the Christian Trinity and Incarnation — to associate any being with God (shirk) is, in Muslim eyes, the most fundamental error.

The line of prophets

Islam recognizes a long chain of prophets — from Adam, Abraham (Ibrahim), and Moses (Musa) to Jesus (Isa), who is honored as a great prophet born of a virgin, but is not God, and was not crucified and resurrected to redeem the world. Muhammad is regarded as the "Seal of the Prophets," the final messenger, sent to correct the revelation that earlier peoples had altered or lost.

Two sources of authority

  • The Qur'an: believed to be God's own speech, revealed word for word in Arabic — the final, unalterable authority; reciting it is itself an act of worship.
  • Hadith and Sunna: the reports of Muhammad's words and deeds, the second source for understanding and living the Qur'an — the vast later system of religious law (Sharia) rests on both.

Eschatology is part of the frame too: belief in the Day of Resurrection, judgment, paradise, and hell — each person answerable to the one God by faith and deed.

Practices & Principles

The Five Pillars: submission made bodily

Islamic devotion does not stay in the heart; it becomes measurable daily practice. Sunni tradition summarizes it as the Five Pillars — five foundational duties shared by all Muslims:

  • Shahada: to witness that "there is no god but God, and Muhammad is the messenger of God" — the doorway and foundation of faith.
  • Salat: five daily prayers facing Mecca, dividing the day into fixed returns to God; a communal congregation gathers at Friday midday.
  • Zakat: giving a set share of surplus wealth (commonly about 2.5%) to the poor — an obligatory, not optional, form of social redistribution.
  • Sawm: during Ramadan, abstaining from food and drink from dawn to sunset — a discipline of self-restraint, empathy with the poor, and focus on God.
  • Hajj: those able in body and means make the pilgrimage to Mecca at least once, where millions in white garments circle the Kaaba — a symbol of the equality of all believers before God.

Everyday ethics also include halal eating (no pork or alcohol), life-cycle rites, and one principle running through all of it: everything returns to the one God, and each person is answerable for their own faith and deeds.

Common Misreadings

Misreading: Islam is "the Arabs' religion."
Arabs are only about a fifth of the world's Muslims. The country with the most Muslims is Indonesia; South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Africa are the demographic center. Islam knows no race.

Misreading: "Allah" is a separate god unique to Islam.
Allah is simply Arabic for "(the one) God" — Arabic-speaking Christians use the same word in prayer. Muslims believe it names the very God worshipped by Abraham, Moses, and Jesus.

Misreading: "jihad" simply means "holy war."
The word means "struggle, striving," and traditionally refers first to the inner effort to master oneself and resist base desires. Its military sense carries strict legal conditions and much debate; it cannot be equated with arbitrary violence.

Misreading: Muslims do not acknowledge Jesus.
On the contrary, the Qur'an honors Jesus (Isa) as a great prophet, born of a virgin, a worker of miracles; the disagreement concerns his "divinity" and "crucifixion for redemption," not his existence or dignity.

Cross-Tradition Reading · The Contemporary Setting

The last link in the Abrahamic chain. Islam shares with Judaism and Christianity the frame of one God, prophets, scripture, and a final judgment, and it too honors Abraham as the father of faith. The key divergence among the three narrows to one point: is God "one to the point of indivisibility" (Islam, Judaism) or "triune and incarnate" (Christianity)? Having read Days 1–5, you will find in Islam many familiar motifs rearranged in a new way.

The closeness to Judaism. Strict monotheism, a ban on idols, an emphasis on law and daily practice (Sharia vs Halakha), dietary rules (halal vs kosher), a faith carried by community rather than priestly hierarchy — structurally, Islam often resembles Judaism more than Christianity.

Tensions today. The historic Sunni–Shia divide (Day 7), the adjustment between traditional law and modern states and human-rights frameworks, Muslim identity in Western societies, and the appropriation of the texts by a small minority of extremists against the rebuttals of mainstream scholars — these are questions no account of contemporary Islam can avoid, and each calls for multiple perspectives.

Going Deeper

Why does Islam see itself as a "return" rather than an "innovation," and what follows from that?
Islam claims not to bring a new God but to correct and seal, one final time, the same monotheistic message forgotten and altered since Adam. This self-understanding has two consequences. First, it naturally folds Judaism and Christianity into the same lineage (calling them "People of the Book," ahl al-kitab), acknowledging kinship while claiming to be the final version. Second, "sealing" means revelation is complete and closed — giving the tradition enormous stability, but also making "how to respond to new circumstances a thousand years later without altering the text" the central and enduring problem of Islamic jurisprudence.
What does it mean for a religion to define faith as five "measurable" duties?
The Five Pillars are practicable, shareable, and reproducible across cultures: whatever the race or language, the gestures and rhythms are largely the same. This lets Islam take root in vastly different societies and shapes a strong sense of the ummah, the global community — fasting in sync, pilgriming to one place, praying in one direction. Read it alongside Jewish commandment-keeping: both write the sacred into body and time rather than leaving it in inner conviction alone. It is worth asking how, when practice is this concrete, the tradition repeatedly stresses the balance between outward observance and inner sincerity (niyya, intention).
In a religion with no priestly hierarchy, where does authority come from?
Sunni Islam in principle has no church and no priestly ranks; a person may stand directly before God. But that does not mean there is no authority: it comes from learning. Scholars (ulama) versed in the Qur'an, Hadith, and law exert guidance through interpretation and consensus (ijma), and different legal schools (madhhab) carry their own traditions. Shia Islam developed a more layered clerical hierarchy. This structure — turning on scholarship rather than ordained office — gives the tradition both flexibility and plurality, and also makes "who is entitled to speak for Islam" a real and open question in the modern age.
